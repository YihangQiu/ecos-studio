\title{ECOS Studio: An RTL-to-Chip Silicon Design Solution with Open-Source EDA, IP, and PDK
}

\begin{abstract}
The development of open chip design ecosystem is limited by 
fragmented open-source EDA tools and the lack of support for mainstream processes and packaging. 
This paper presents ECOS Studio, an integrated silicon design solution orchestrating a robust open-source EDA
toolchain, an open IP library, and an open 55 nm PDK. One of its key features is an RTL-to-chip service model that automatically handles
complexities in the supply chain of chip design. Its efficacy has been validated through the successful tape-out of a RISC-V chip and its 
deployment in a massive-scale chip design education project. 

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}


The open hardware movement has fundamentally reshaped the landscape of IC design over the past decade,
which reduces costs for the industry and lowers the research and educational barrier for engineers and students. 
While open-standard ISAs like RISC-V have broken down the proprietary walls of processors, establishing a complete open IC ecosystem necessitates 
two essential components: robust open-source EDA toolchains and accessible PDKs. Despite progress in academia and industry represented by 
OpenROAD~\cite{2019openroad} and legacy node open PDKs (\eg 130~nm)~\cite{ihp130}, integrating them into a cohesive, 
industrial-strength chip design flow remains a significant challenge. 

Current open design flows are mainly hindered by three limitations. 
First, the \emph{fragmentation and poor usability of open-source EDA ecosystem} force engineers to struggle with 
separate tools and complex environment configurations rather than focusing on architectural design. 
Second, a \emph{lack of support for mainstream process nodes} confines designs to legacy processes that are unable to meet 
high performance demands, whereas mainstream nodes are largely inaccessible to open tools due to proprietary constraints. 
Third, the \emph{absence of downstream packaging} is a last-mile gap that obstructs real-world validation of the designed systems.

To address these challenges, this paper presents \textbf{ECOS Studio}\footnote{\url{https://github.com/openecos-projects/ecos-studio}}, 
an out-of-the-box, one-stop chip design solution democratizing 
access to custom silicon. By vertically integrating a verified open IP library, an EDA toolchain named \textbf{ECC}, and a 55~nm PDK into a
unified framework, ECOS Studio automates the design flow and reduces the complexity of chip design from an ``expert-only'' domain to a level 
accessible by junior engineers, researchers, and students. The main contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:

\emph{a) Full-Stack RTL-to-Chip Service:}
We propose an one-stop service model that abstracts the chip design supply chain from 
logical and physical implementation to chip packaging. It allows users to focus on RTL design and receive functional 
silicon directly, offering an FPGA-like experience for ASICs.

\emph{b) 55 nm Process Enablement:}
We bridge the gap between open-source tools and industrial standards by adapting the ECC toolchain 
for a commercial 55~nm process. The flow is silicon-proven via a SoC tape-out that passed commercial DRC/LVS sign-off.

\emph{c) Automated MPW Aggregation:}
To further lower the barrier to entry, we integrate an automated Multi-Project Wafer (MPW) aggregation mechanism, capable of merging 
multiple user designs into shared reticles. It amortizes design costs, making large-scale silicon research and education affordable.

\section{ECOS Studio}

This section introduces ECOS Studio, a holistic solution that follows the RTL-to-chip service paradigm, 
bridging the gap between RTL design and physical realization. As illustrated in \cref{fig:layout_combined}, ECOS Studio takes a user's
RTL code and manages all downstream complexities, ultimately delivering a functional chip to the user.
This service is currently provided through a locally installable software, with a planned cloud-deployed version to improve accessibility and ease of use. 
ECOS Studio is built upon three essential open-source pillars to enable the RTL-to-chip service model.

\subsection{Open-Source IP: Infrastructure for Composable Design}
To support the RTL-to-chip objective, we construct a comprehensive open-access IP infrastructure comprising two core components. 
First, a free IP repository provides pre-verified soft IPs (\eg UART, SPI) and critical foundry IPs (\eg SRAMs, PLLs) that are 
typically gate-kept under NDAs in commercial flows. Second, a configurable SoC template acts as a reliable skeleton consisting of
a pre-validated RISC-V processor core and a hierarchical bus interconnect. 
It enables a ``mix-and-match'' workflow where users can freely select
IPs from the repository and couple them with the template, ensuring that the assembled design is strictly compliant with the 55~nm physical rules.

\subsection{Open-Source EDA: The ECC Toolchain}
ECOS Studio is powered by ECC, a toolchain optimized for \emph{solution maturity} rather than merely algorithmic performance. 
ECC integrates discrete EDA tools into a seamless RTL-to-GDSII pipeline that prioritizes engineering robustness, 
achieving low crash rates on large-scale designs and ensuring deterministic outputs. 
Furthermore, ECC leverages containerization to encapsulate the entire toolchain into a unified Docker image and to decouple the flow from local OS dependencies, 
which is a common issue of traditional EDA software. 
The cloud version of ECOS Studio is expected to support thousands of concurrent jobs and will enable 
users to access a full-featured chip design environment via a simple web browser.

\subsection{Open-Source PDK: Enabling Mainstream 55 nm Process}
To enable mainstream manufacturing, ECOS Studio adopts the ICsprout 55 nm Open PDK~\cite{ics55} released in 2025. 
As shown in \cref{tab:tech_comparison}, this technology represents a strategic sweet spot for open chips. 
It enables faster and more cost-efficient designs while avoiding complex layout-dependent effects in advanced nodes. 
Notably, the 55 nm PDK achieves an 85\% reduction in cell area compared to the legacy IHP 130~nm~\cite{ihp130} node, 
delivering industrial-level density with acceptable costs for academic MPWs.

\section{Applications}

This section presents real-world achievements of ECOS Studio to demonstrate its effectiveness, including a successful tape-out
and empowering chip design education.

\subsection{Solution of Fast, Low-cost Chip Design and Tape-outs}

To perform functional validation of the open-source flow, we design RetroSoC~\cite{retrosoc}, a fully open-source ASIC
integrating an RV32IMAC core with peripherals including PSRAM controller, UART, and I2C. ECC is used for implementation. 
The tape-out adopts the MPW shuttle mode, completing GDSII sign-off in June, 2025 and receiving silicon in December, 2025.
The chip operates correctly, achieving a max frequency of 100 MHz, with a die area of 4 $mm^2$ and 1.517 millions of gates.

After the successful RetroSoC tape-out, we launch the second tape-out focusing on low costs. We introduce an automated GDS
aggregation method that integrate multiple user designs in to a single die with shared I/Os. Each design is hardened
as a macro and placed into a shared floorplan, and a global multiplexing logic is synthesized to route the shared I/O pads to
the active user design. Compared to traditional MPW where each design occupies its own pad frame, our strategy allows the
integrated designs to share a single I/O ring, thus improving silicon area utilization and lowering per-design cost. 


\subsection{Platform for Mass Education of Chip Design}

``One Student One Chip'' (OSOC)~\cite{osoc} is a pioneering project~targeting mass education of chip design, breaking the traditional
barriers of universities and majors. It has constructed a large-scale knowledge infrastructure as curriculum materials
which are released under open licences, and has attracted over 16,000 participants as of 2025. 

While OSOC successfully popularizes front-end design knowledge, the barrier to back-end implementation for thousands of 
participants remains high. To tackle this, ECOS Studio is being integrated into the OSOC infrastructure. After deployment, 
ECOS Studio can serve as a zero-configuration EDA environment to all OSOC participants, enabling them to seamlessly convert
their learned knowledge into layouts or even functional chips, thus completing the RTL-to-chip objective that
was previously impossible at this scale.


\section{Conclusion and Outlook}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper presents ECOS Studio, a vertically integrated, out-of-the-box silicon design solution that democratizes access to custom chips. By orchestrating the ECC toolchain, open IPs, and a 55 nm-compatible PDK, we have bridged the gap between academic projects and industrial manufacturing. The RTL-to-chip service model and automated MPW aggregation mechanism have proven effective in both the rigorous silicon validation of RetroSoC and the massive-scale talent cultivation of the OSOC project.

We believe that the silicon industry is about to experience a transition similar to the recent paradigm shift in the AI industry with
the emergence of high-performance open large language models. Previously, open-source EDA tools were largely confined to the capabilities
of MCU-level designs that are typically limited to 100K gate count and 100 MHz frequency. With the enablement of mainstream commercial
PDKs and the continuous improvement of open-source design tools, CPU-level designs targeting 1M gate count and 1 GHz frequency are 
no longer impossible. The convergence of open EDA, IP, and PDK will start a new era where custom silicon design becomes 
as ubiquitous and agile as software development.
